The Gentrification of Gowanus

What's going on in Gowanus, really?

Background

Artists involved in the 1980s art scene found it hard to find affordable loft space in Manhattan, so they chose to move to Gowanus. Gowanus' industrial lofts were like those in SoHo, but much more affordable for these people1.

Salvatore Scotto, a community advocate, promoted Gowanus to developers and potential residents, who became interested in the area, by holding neighborhood tours. He networked with developers, who would renovate vacant properties for profit. These new apartments were advertised to wealthy Manhattan residents2.

Lifelong Gowanus residents didn't trust Scotto. They argued that Scotto's “marketing” of the neighborhood was causing the gentrification that pushed lifelong residents out of their homes. Celia Cacace, Scotto's most vocal critic at the time, stated, "I don't call it gentrification, I call it genocide because they're killing off neighborhoods."3

In 2008, a real estate development company, the Toll Brothers, proposed a plan to build luxury apartment complexes next to the canal, even though it's not safe for people to live there. The Toll Brothers believed that the development would "spur the cleanup" of the canal thanks to the incoming wealthy condo buyers4. Toll Brothers abandoned the plan in 2010, after the Gowanus Canal was declared a Superfund site.5

Green Gentrification

What is greening?

Greening6 is:

  • The process of becoming more active about protecting the environment
  • The process of making somewhere greener by planting grass, trees, and plants there
  • The process of beginning to pay attention to the protection of the natural environment

Greening is used to improve neighborhoods where low-income people and people of color live by creating or restoring environmental amenities such as parks. However, greening can also attract wealthier and/or white residents, who push out lower-income and minority residents7.

Who benefits from greening?

In the end, the people who get to enjoy these green changes the most are the new wealthier residents, who also tend to be in positions of greater power8.

How is greening related to gentrification?

The negative result of greening, which leads to the exclusion and displacement of politically disenfranchised residents9, is recognized as a form of gentrification known as green gentrification.

Just as how greening can lead to gentrification, gentrification in an area can also lead to greening10.

Gentrification

What is happening in Gowanus?

Real estate development in Gowanus has been prominent since the early 2000s. Gowanus residents believed that the area's pollution would deter luxury development from taking place11, but this wasn't the case. Real estate developers have paid several million dollars for empty and abandoned lots in the area, with plans to build “hip” apartment complexes.

General public interest in the cleanup of the Gowanus Canal only began when real estate development in the area started and wealthier people started moving in. The cleanup, or greening, of the Gowanus Canal encourages real estate developers to invest in the area, which raises housing prices even more12.

Gowanus' median home price in 2022 is $1.79 million, compared to $630,000 in 2009. Average rent jumped from $1,900 in 2009 to $3,910 in 202213, 14, 15.

Why is gentrification a problem?

Gentrification pushes out longtime residents, most of who are unable to benefit from the positive changes to the neighborhood. It can also change the culture and character of a neighborhood completely, often against residents' will.
