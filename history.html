Amazon is Known to Mistreat Their Workers

  • The median annual pay for Amazon workers is $28,446.

    Most of Amazon's employees work in fulfillment centers.

    The median annual pay at Facebook is $240,430.

  • Amazon uses an app to track and discipline delivery drivers.

    Amazon requires contracted delivery drivers to download an app, called Mentor, that tracks and scores their driving behavior.

    Even though the app is supposed to improve driver safety, Amazon employees say that the technology produces errors and can even track their location after they're done working.

  • Amazon has policies that penalize workers for taking too many breaks.

    Amazon tracks employee productivity rates, which puts them at greater risk of exposure to Covid-19.

  • Amazon attempted to force workers planning to unionize at an Alabama warehouse to vote in person.

    Amazon argued mail-in ballots would take too long and involve too many resources.

  • Amazon's 45 minute long anti-union training video.

    The video is specifically designed, according to the narrator, to "give you the tools that you need for success when it comes to labor organizing."

    Actual lines from the video: "We do not believe unions are in the best interest of our customers, our shareholders, or most importantly, our associates. Our business model is built upon speed, innovation, and customer obsession—things that are generally not associated with union. When we lose sight of those critical focus areas we jeopardize everyone's job security: yours, mine, and the associates'."

  • Amazon doesn't take responsibility when their drivers are involved in serious accidents.

    Amazon's fast shipping can have deadly consequences, even for those who have nothing to do with the company.

  • Amazon runs a digital work marketplace called Mechanical Turk.

    Freelance workers complete tasks for pennies.

    Most people earn under $5 an hour. Some make that much in a day.

  • Extreme quotas at Amazon warehouses can lead to injury or even death.

    At 23 of Amazon's facilities nationwide, the rate of serious injuries was more than double the national average for the warehousing industry. in 2018, there were 9.6 injuries per 100 employees, compared to the industry average of 4.

    "When you order something from Amazon and you’ve worked inside Amazon, you wonder, 'Hey, is ordering my package going to be the demise of somebody?'" said one former safety manager, who had worked at multiple Amazon facilities.

  • When Amazon raised the minimum wage for warehouse workers to $15, they also got rid of monthly bonuses and stock awards.

    Amazon claimed the wage increase "more than compensates" for the loss of other benefits, such as incentive pay and stock option awards.

  • A Chicago warehouse's Juneteenth "celebration."

    At a Chicago warehouse, the facility offered an "authentic meal" of chicken and waffles in celebration of Juneteenth.

    Employees said they were angered and insulted by the racist form of celebration.

  • Amazon fired two employees who called for climate change reform and better warehouse conditions.

    UX designers Emily Cunningham and Maren Costa, two leaders of the Amazon Employees for Climate Justice group, were fired for "repeatedly violating internal policies."

  • A May 2020 Covid-19 outbreak at a Minnesota Amazon warehouse was worse than the local county's.

    Covid-19 cases at the warehouse exceeded the infection rate of surrounding communities by at least 4 times.

  • Amazon front-line workers' $300 bonuses.

    To celebrate the holidays last year, full-time employees received a $300 bonus, while part-time workers received a $150 bonus. This was a one-time bonus.

  • Amazon's low pay rate leaves workers struggling.

    Many Amazon employees struggle to pay bills such as rent, food, utilities, and the cost of commuting.

    Over 8,000 employees in 11 states are on food stamps, which taxpayers pay for.

  • Amazon warehouse security screenings.

    Workers at Amazon warehouses have to go through a security screening at the end of the day. The screenings are meant to prevent theft.

    The workers aren't paid for the screenings, which can take as long as 25 minutes.

  • Amazon takes tips from its delivery drivers.

    Amazon didn't pass on tips to drivers for more than two years and "took elaborate steps to mislead its drivers and conceal its theft, sending them canned responses that repeated the company’s lies."

    Amazon took $61.7 million in tips from drivers, which they then had to pay in a settlement.

  • The megacycle shifts.

    Amazon workers at a Chicago warehouse had to choose between taking a ten-and-a-half hour graveyard shift (running from 1:20 am to 11:50 am) or losing their jobs.

    "Amazon's nationwide push to move its workers from shorter daytime shifts to longer shifts that begin in the middle of the night fits within the company's efforts to increase efficiency and speed up delivery speed for its customers at the cost of its workers' health and safety."
