History of the Gowanus Canal

Gowanus has a rich history, so here are some key events

Early History

Gowanus Creek, a tidal estuary, emerged around 1,500 years ago1. A tidal estuary is a partially enclosed body of saltwater. Estuaries don't have currents, aside from the movement of the tides. The streams that extended from the creek were surrounded by a salt marsh.

Members of the Lenni-Lenape group of Algonquin Native Americans were the earliest occupants of the land around the Gowanus Creek. They relied on the area's natural resources, including fish, shellfish, and corn2.

Gowanus' namesake comes from Gouwane, a Lenape chief3. The name “Gouwane” can be translated to “he rests” or “the sleeper.”4

Gowanus and its surrounding areas are one of the earliest sites of outside settlement in America. In 1636, two Englishmen, William Adrianse Bennet and Jacques Bentin, bought land in Gowanus from Sachem Ka, a Lenni-Lenape chief5. That same year, Brooklyn's first Dutch settlers made the Gowanus Bay their home6.

The First Tide Mill

Gowanus' first tide mill (called Brouwer's Mill, Freeke's Mill, or the Old Gowanus Mill) was built around 1661 by Adam Brouwer. The mill functioned with the help of millponds that were dug out by slaves. Millponds controlled by a gate were necessary to produce functioning mills. The millpond filled up at high tide. At low tide, the gate opened, letting water flow out. This water made the millstone turn7.

Initial Canal Plans

By the 1840s, Gowanus Creek seemed to have reached a state of obsolescence. Modern boats were unable to pass since it was too shallow, and the land around the creek flooded whenever it rained8. A new challenge at the time was figuring out how to stop the land from flooding. An engineer, Daniel Richards, proposed digging basins but was unable to get the political and financial support he needed to make this a reality. Even though he was unable to complete his proposal, his design determined what the Gowanus Canal would look like in the present day9.

Gowanus' Sewers

As Brooklyn's population increased in the 1850s, so did their waste and needs. Brooklynites needed working sewers. Julius W. Adams, an engineer, proposed a combined sewer. Water runoff and household sewage would be collected in the same pipes, which was then discarded into a body of saltwater10.

Eventually, household waste was dumped directly into the Gowanus Canal, which flooded when it rained and emitted an awful stench that managed to kill people11.

Construction of the Canal and Industrialization

In 1849, New York State's government approved the cleaning out of Gowanus Creek to build a canal. Major David Douglas of the Army Corp of Engineers was hired to design the Gowanus Canal in 186712. Construction began that same year and was completed in 1869. At the time, people were not aware that the canal, an inland waterway with no outlet, would eventually have negative effects on water quality and human health13.

Thanks to the newly constructed canal, the area became a key center for local, regional, and national industrial growth14. The canal's waterfront became home to warehouses, coal gas manufacturing plants, cement works, breweries, chemical plants, tanneries, and paper mills15. These industries dumped their waste into the canal.

The Gowanus Expressway

After World War II, national commercial transportation shifted from rail and ships to trucks. The Gowanus Expressway connected Gowanus to the interstate highway system but destroyed local homes and businesses16. Pollution in the area also increased due to the number of vehicles that crossed over the expressway daily17. The construction of the Gowanus Expressway killed off Gowanus' remaining industries, and the canal was abandoned18.
